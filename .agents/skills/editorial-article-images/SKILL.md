# Editorial Article Images

## Purpose

Create article images that look designed, printed, and intentional: mid-century editorial poster language reinterpreted for contemporary essays, newsletters, and longform web writing. Prefer tactile illustration, strong silhouettes, warm paper texture, geometric abstraction, and human-scale metaphors.

Do not aim for photorealism, generative-AI gloss, stock editorial sameness, or tech cliche. Images should support writing, not overpower it. They may be used as hero images, inline illustrations, section breaks, social previews, or article cards.

## Palette First

Before writing prompts, ask the user what color palette they want.

If the user already provides a palette, use it directly and keep the image to no more than four main colors. If they do not know, offer 3-5 concise palette directions instead of defaulting silently.

Example palette options:

- Warm editorial: off-white paper, near-black ink, vermillion, muted cream.
- Crisp modern: off-white paper, cobalt blue, near-black, forest green.
- Earthy systems: tan paper, deep teal, forest green, muted cream.
- Tension and contrast: off-white paper, near-black, cobalt blue, vermillion.
- Quiet literary: cream paper, charcoal, faded blue, warm ochre.

Once a palette is chosen, include it explicitly in every image prompt.

Avoid one-note palettes. Use restrained contrast, but make sure the image has enough distinction to work at article-card and social-preview sizes.

## Prompt Formula

Start with this structure and adapt subject, composition, palette, and article placement:

```text
A vintage-modern editorial illustration of [subject/metaphor], composed for [article use case: hero image / inline essay illustration / article card / social preview], with [negative-space direction if needed], [chosen color palette]. Bold simplified shapes, subtle risograph grain, screenprint texture, crisp edges, poetic humanist mood, no photorealism, no UI, no technology cliches, no text, no logo.
```

Add concrete layout instructions:

```text
Leave the upper third mostly open so the image can sit beneath an article title.
```

```text
Place the main subject on the right third, leaving the left half calm and uncluttered.
```

```text
Center the object low on the canvas with lots of quiet paper space above.
```

```text
Make the composition readable as a small article thumbnail as well as a large hero image.
```

## Image Direction

Always preserve breathing room. Article images often sit near headlines, decks, captions, author metadata, or social-card crops, so explicitly request open zones: "leave the top third mostly open," "keep the left 45% quiet," "subject occupies the lower right quadrant," or "avoid important details near the edges."

Use no more than four main colors in one image.

Texture should be subtle: risograph grain, screenprint halftone softness, slight paper tooth, imperfect ink edges, and very light vintage poster wear. Do not make it dirty, grungy, or distressed unless asked.

Prefer simple metaphors:

- Bicycle: balance, human-scale systems, agency.
- Cliff/horizon: uncertainty, choice, future.
- Rings/loops: play, iteration, experimentation.
- Tree: conditions, growth, environment.
- Doorway: transition.
- Compass: agency and direction.
- Kite: freedom within form.
- Bridge: connection between human and machine.
- Workshop tools: craft and judgment.
- Sailboat: exploration, navigation, uncertainty.
- Lantern: attention, discernment, seeing clearly.
- Table/workbench: practice, craft, collaboration.
- Garden/greenhouse: conditions, care, emergence.

Never request or accept images centered on robots, robot hands, glowing neural networks, circuit boards, holograms, blue tech panels, generic AI brains, chatbot bubbles, dashboards, futuristic UI, laptops, corporate meeting rooms, stock photography, glossy 3D renders, cyberpunk, fake text, logos, watermarks, or baked-in labels.

If using a brain/tree/system metaphor, make it stylized and poster-like, not medical, sci-fi, or literal tech.

## Article Use Cases

### Hero Image

For the top of an article:

```text
A vintage-modern editorial illustration of [subject/metaphor], composed as a wide article hero image, with the main subject placed low and slightly right, leaving the upper left calm and open for surrounding article typography, using [chosen palette]. Bold simplified shapes, warm paper texture, subtle risograph grain, crisp poster style, no text, no logo, no photorealism.
```

### Inline Illustration

For a section break or concept image inside the article:

```text
A compact vintage-modern editorial illustration of [concept/metaphor], composed as an inline essay image with a centered subject and generous paper margins, using [chosen palette]. Simple geometric forms, tactile screenprint texture, poetic but restrained mood, no text, no logo, no UI, no photorealism.
```

### Article Card

For an index page, related post card, or preview tile:

```text
A bold vintage-modern editorial illustration of [subject/metaphor], composed for a small article thumbnail, simple silhouette readable at small size, minimal background detail, using [chosen palette]. Strong shape language, subtle print grain, no text, no logo, no photorealism.
```

### Social Preview

For Open Graph or newsletter preview images:

```text
A vintage-modern editorial poster illustration of [subject/metaphor], composed for a 16:9 social preview image, with the subject occupying the right third and the left half visually quiet, using [chosen palette]. Bold simplified shapes, subtle risograph grain, crisp edges, no text, no logo, no UI, no photorealism.
```


```text

```

```text

```

```text
Agency: A tiny human figure standing near a doorway, bridge, or horizon line, minimal forms, dramatic negative space, vintage-modern editorial illustration, using [chosen palette], no text, no logo.
```

## Integration

After generating or selecting an image:

1. Crop with intention; never stretch.
2. Keep headlines, labels, slogans, and captions as real typography outside the image.
3. Match the article context: wide hero, inline figure, square card, or 16:9 social preview.
4. Preserve quiet space around the subject so the writing remains primary.
5. Use texture to complement the article voice, not compete with it.
6. Check that the image still reads clearly when cropped for article cards and social previews.
