The technical part of rolling out a new system is usually finished long before the system is actually adopted. The gap between those two moments is filled with people, and that is the part that gets under-resourced because it does not produce a commit.

I have started treating that gap as the actual project.

## What goes wrong, predictably

- The people who understood the old way are treated as obstacles instead of as the institution's memory. They leave, and the memory leaves with them.
- The new system is introduced as a fact, not a negotiation. People comply, route around it, and the real workflow goes underground where you cannot improve it.
- Judgement is automated away to look efficient, and the first non-standard case proves why it should not have been.

## What helps

Facilitation that is boring on purpose. Naming what the change costs, out loud, before the launch. Keeping a human accountable for outcomes rather than letting the tool absorb the accountability. Designing the correction path before the happy path, so being wrong is survivable.

> Adoption that costs people their judgement, standing, or memory is not adoption. It is attrition with a launch date.

None of this shows up in the changelog. It is the difference between a system that was installed and a system that is used, which is the only difference that ever mattered.
